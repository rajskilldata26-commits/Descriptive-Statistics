Question 1: A die is rolled. What is the probability of getting:
(a) An even number
(b) A number greater than 4

Sol:-A fair die has 6 equally likely outcomes: {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.

(a) Probability of getting an even number
Even numbers are: {2, 4, 6} → 3 favorable outcomes
Total outcomes = 6
Probability = 3/6 = 1/2

b) Probability of getting a number greater than 4
Numbers greater than 4 are: {5, 6} → 2 favorable outcomes
Total outcomes = 6
Probability = 2/6 = 1/3

Question 2: In a class of 50 students:
(A)20 like Mathematics (M)
(B)15 like Science (S)
(C)5 like both subjects
What is the probability that a student chosen at random likes Mathematics or Science?

Sol:-To find the probability that a student likes Mathematics or Science (M ∪ S), we use the inclusion–exclusion principle:
P(M∪S)=n(M)+n(S)−n(M∩S)/Total
Now substitute the values:
n(M)=20
n(S)=15
n(M∩S)=5
Total students = 50
P(M∪S)=20+15−5/50=30/50=3/5

Final Answer:
The probability = 3/5

Question 3: A bag has 3 red and 2 blue balls. If one ball is drawn randomly and is red, what is the probability
that the next ball is also red (without replacement)?

 Sol:-Since the first ball drawn is red, we update the situation:
Initially: 3 red, 2 blue (total 5)
After drawing one red (and not replacing it):
Remaining → 2 red, 2 blue (total 4)
Now, the probability that the next ball is also red:
P(second red∣first red)=2/4=1/2

Final Answer:
The probability is 1/2

Question 4: The population of a school is divided into 60% boys and 40% girls. If you want equal representation
of both genders in the sample, which method should you use: Simple Random Sampling or Stratified Sampling?
Why?

Sol:-You should use Stratified Sampling.
Why?
In the population, boys (60%) and girls (40%) are not equally represented. If you use Simple Random Sampling, you might end up with more boys than girls in your sample just by chance.
With Stratified Sampling, you divide the population into two groups (strata): boys and girls, and then select an equal number from each group. This guarantees equal representation of both genders in your sample.
Final Answer:
Use Stratified Sampling, because it ensures balanced representation from both groups.

Question 5: The average height of 1000 students = 160 cm. A sample of 100 students shows an average height
= 158 cm.
Find the sampling error.

Sol:-Sampling error is the difference between the sample mean and the population mean.
Sampling Error= xˉ−μ
Here:
Population mean μ=160 cm
Sample mean xˉ=158 cm
Sampling Error=158−160=−2 cm
Final Answer:
Sampling error = −2 cm (the sample underestimates the population mean by 2 cm).

Question 7: In a group of 100 students:
40 like Cricket (C)
30 like Football (F)
10 like both Cricket and Football
Find the probability that a student likes at least one sport.

Sol:-We want the probability that a student likes at least one sport (Cricket or Football), i.e., C∪F.
Using the inclusion–exclusion principle:
          P(C∪F)=n(C)+n(F)−n(C∩F)/Total
Substitute the values:
n(C)=40
n(F)=30
n(C∩F)=10
Total students = 100
P(C∪F) = 40+30−10/100 
       = 60/100=3/5
Final Answer:
The probability = 3/5

Question 10: Differentiate between discrete and continuous random variables with examples.

Sol:-The difference comes down to the type of values the variable can take.

Discrete Random Variable
Takes countable, separate values (usually whole numbers).
You can list all possible outcomes.
Often comes from counting.
Examples:
Number of students in a class
Number of heads when tossing 3 coins
Number of cars passing a signal 
Values look like: 0, 1, 2, 3, … (no fractions in between)

Continuous Random Variable
Takes any value within a range (including decimals).
Cannot list all possible values because there are infinitely many.
Comes from measurement.
Examples:
Height of students (e.g., 160.5 cm, 172.3 cm)
Time taken to complete a task
Temperature of a room
Values can be: 1.2, 1.25, 1.253… (infinite possibilities)

Key Difference
Discrete: Countable values (finite or countably infinite)
Continuous: Measurable values (uncountably infinite)

In short:
Counting → Discrete
Measuring → Continuous
