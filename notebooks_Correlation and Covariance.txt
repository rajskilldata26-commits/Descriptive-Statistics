Q2. What does a positive, negative, and zero covariance indicate about the relationship between two variables?
SOL:-Covariance tells us how two variables move together. The sign of covariance gives the direction of their relationship:
1. Positive Covariance
Indicates that both variables move in the same direction
When one variable increases, the other also tends to increase (and vice versa)
Example: Height and weight ‚ÄĒ generally, taller people tend to weigh more

2. Negative Covariance
Indicates that variables move in opposite directions
When one variable increases, the other tends to decrease
Example: Price and demand ‚ÄĒ as price increases, demand usually decreases

3. Zero Covariance
Indicates no linear relationship between the variables
Changes in one variable do not show any consistent pattern with the other
Note: It does not always mean complete independence, just no linear association

Q3. Discuss the limitations of covariance as a measure of relationship between two variables. Why is correlation
preferred in many cases?
SOL:-Covariance is useful for indicating whether two variables move together, but it has several important limitations that make it less practical as a standalone measure.

Limitations of Covariance
1. Depends on scale (units)
Covariance is affected by the units of measurement (e.g., meters, rupees, kg).
If you change units (like cm ‚Üí meters), the covariance value changes.
This makes comparisons across datasets difficult.

2. No fixed range
Covariance can take any value from ‚ąí‚ąě to +‚ąě.
Because there is no standard range, it‚Äôs hard to judge whether a value is ‚Äústrong‚ÄĚ or ‚Äúweak.‚ÄĚ

3. Hard to interpret magnitude
While the sign (positive/negative) is meaningful, the size of covariance doesn‚Äôt clearly indicate the strength of the relationship.
A value like 500 may be large or small depending on the data scale.

4. Not standardized
Covariance does not provide a relative measure of association.
You cannot directly compare covariance values across different variable pairs.

Why Correlation is Preferred
Correlation overcomes these issues by standardizing covariance:

1. Unit-free (dimensionless)
 Correlation removes the effect of units, making it easier to compare relationships.

2. Fixed range (-1 to +1)
 Values are always between -1 and +1, making interpretation straightforward.

3. Measures strength and direction
Clearly shows both:
 Direction (positive/negative)
 Strength (weak/moderate/strong)

4. Comparable across datasets
Because it is standardized, correlation allows meaningful comparison between different variables and studies.

Q4. Explain the difference between Pearson‚Äôs correlation coefficient and Spearman‚Äôs rank correlation
coefficient. When would you prefer to use Spearman‚Äôs correlation?
SOL:-Pearson‚Äôs correlation coefficient and Spearman‚Äôs rank correlation coefficient are both used to measure the relationship between two variables, but they differ in how they work and when they should be used.

Pearson‚Äôs Correlation Coefficient (r)
Measures the linear relationship between two continuous variables
Uses the actual numerical values
Assumes:
Data is normally distributed (approximately)
Relationship is linear
Sensitive to outliers (extreme values can distort results)
Interpretation:
+1 ‚Üí perfect positive linear relationship
0 ‚Üí no linear relationship
-1 ‚Üí perfect negative linear relationship

Spearman‚Äôs Rank Correlation Coefficient (ŌĀ or rs)
Measures the monotonic relationship (whether variables move in the same/opposite direction, not necessarily linearly)
Uses ranks instead of actual values
Does not require normal distribution
Less sensitive to outliers
Interpretation:
 +1 ‚Üí perfect increasing (monotonic) relationship
 0 ‚Üí no monotonic relationship
 -1 ‚Üí perfect decreasing relationship

| Feature                 | Pearson            | Spearman                           |
| ----------------------- | ------------------ | ---------------------------------- |
| Type of relationship    | Linear             | Monotonic (not necessarily linear) |
| Data used               | Actual values      | Ranks                              |
| Sensitivity to outliers | High               | Low                                |
| Assumption of normality | Required (approx.) | Not required                       |

When to Prefer Spearman‚Äôs Correlation
Use Spearman‚Äôs correlation when:
The relationship is not linear but still ordered (e.g., steadily increasing curve)
Data contains outliers
Data is ordinal (ranked data, like class positions)
The dataset is not normally distributed
The relationship is monotonic but nonlinear
Example
 If marks and ranks are compared ‚Üí use Spearman
 If height and weight are compared (linear trend) ‚Üí use Pearson

Q5. If the correlation coefficient between two variables X and Y is 0.85, interpret this value in context. Can you
infer causation from this value? Why or why not?
SOL:-A correlation coefficient of 0.85 indicates a strong positive relationship between variables X and Y.
Interpretation
The value 0.85 is close to +1, so:
As X increases, Y also tends to increase
The relationship is strong and fairly consistent
In practical terms, the data points would lie close to an upward-sloping straight line, though not perfectly on it

Can we infer causation?
No, correlation does not imply causation.
Why not?

1. Direction is unclear
Correlation does not tell us whether:
X causes Y
Y causes X
or both influence each other

2. Third variable problem
A hidden variable (confounder) may be influencing both X and Y
Example: Ice cream sales and drowning incidents are correlated, but both are influenced by temperature

3. Coincidental relationship
Sometimes variables show high correlation purely by chance, without any real connection
